## Preface

## The moral of the entrepreneur.

### Alexander's Life

Born in 356 B.C. in Pella, the capital of Macedon, Alexander was the son of King Philip II, a ruler who transformed a peripheral kingdom into the dominant military power of Greece.[1][2] From an early age, Alexander was exposed to both conquest and ambition as daily realities. His father expanded territory relentlessly; his mother, Olympias, is said to have told him that his true father was not Philip II of Macedon, but Zeus himself.[2] Other traditions linked him to Achilles through his maternal lineage, and even to Heracles through his father’s line.[2]

At thirteen, he was placed under the tutelage of Aristotle.[2][3] Under Aristotle, Alexander studied philosophy, science, medicine, and literature. He read Homer obsessively, carrying a copy of the Iliad with him on campaigns, modeling himself after Achilles.[2] Plutarch writes that Alexander called the *Iliad* "a portable treasury of military virtue and knowledge."[2] This was not casual admiration, it was instruction. He was not preparing to rule; he was preparing to become a figure worth writing about.

At sixteen, while Philip campaigned abroad, Alexander was left in charge of Macedon as regent. During this time, he suppressed a rebellion and founded a city, Alexandropolis, marking his first act of both destruction and creation.[1][2] At eighteen, he commanded cavalry at the Battle of Chaeronea, where Macedon decisively defeated the Greek city-states.[1] Alexander led the charge that broke the Sacred Band of Thebes, one of the most elite military units in Greece.[1][4] He did not inherit a kingdom passively-he earned his place in it through visible, decisive action.

When Philip II was assassinated in 336 B.C., Alexander ascended to the throne at just twenty years old.[1][2] Immediately, he faced internal instability and external threats. Greek city-states saw an opportunity to revolt. Alexander responded without hesitation: he marched south, crushed rebellions, and destroyed Thebes entirely as a warning.[1][4] Athens was spared, but the message was clear-there would be no fragmentation.

With Greece secured, Alexander turned to the Persian Empire, the largest and wealthiest empire of the time. Crossing into Asia Minor in 334 B.C., he began a campaign that would redefine the scale of conquest.[3][5] Victories at the Battle of Granicus, the Battle of Issus, and later Gaugamela systematically dismantled Persian power.[3][5] Each battle was not just a win-it was a statement of inevitability.

In the city of Gordium stood an ancient cart tied with an intricate knot, known as the Gordian Knot. Legend held that whoever could untie it would become ruler of all Asia.[3][6] Many had tried and failed. The knot was dense, complex, and seemingly impossible to unravel through conventional means. Alexander examined it briefly. Then, instead of attempting to untie it, he drew his sword and cut through it in a single stroke (one of multiple ancient versions of the story).[3][6]

Alexander moved fast. Not recklessly, but decisively. He adapted tactics mid-battle, leveraged terrain, and often placed himself at the most dangerous points of engagement.[3][5] His presence was not symbolic; it was operational. He was seen fighting, leading charges, taking wounds.[3][5] This built an army that did not just follow orders-it followed belief.

After defeating Darius III, he did not stop. He pushed deeper into Egypt, where he was declared a pharaoh, and then further east into Central Asia and India.[1][3] He founded over twenty cities, many named Alexandria, establishing not just military control but cultural and economic nodes.[1][5] Greek language and ideas spread across vast territories, merging with local cultures into what would later be known as the Hellenistic world.[1][5]

His campaigns stretched supply lines to their limits, tested the loyalty of his troops, and introduced constant friction between ambition and sustainability.[3][5] Eventually, his army refused to march further east in India.[3] For the first time, Alexander had to stop-not because he was defeated, but because momentum alone could not override human limits.

He died in 323 B.C. in Babylon at the age of 32.[1][3] In just over a decade, he had created one of the largest empires in history, stretching from Greece to northwestern India.[1][5]

### Alexander: Ethics

The defining trait of Alexander the Great is not strategy, nor courage, but a form of ambition that resists rational explanation.

As the son of a king, his dominant incentive should have been preservation. Doing nothing meant ruling Macedon, living in security, enjoying status, and avoiding existential risk. Attacking the Achaemenid Persian Empire meant the opposite: uncertainty, constant danger, and a high probability of failure or death. A strictly rational actor would have stayed.

Alexander did not.

He repeatedly chose paths where the downside was catastrophic and the upside was historically absurd. This is not calculated risk-taking in the modern sense—it is closer to a rejection of the baseline itself.

His behavior reinforces this pattern. He fought on the front lines, exposed himself to injury, and shared conditions with his soldiers.[3][5] The well-known anecdote of him refusing water in the desert until all his men could drink illustrates something deeper than discipline: a deliberate alignment with hardship rather than avoidance of it.[3]

Arrian describes the scene this way:

> "Alexander took the helmet and thanked him, but did not drink. He poured the water on the ground in full view of his troops."
>
> — Arrian, *Anabasis*, Book VI

The most symbolic example is the Gordian Knot. Faced with a problem designed to be solved through patience and technique, he cut through it. Not because it was optimal, but because he refused to operate within the constraints of the problem itself.

Even in decisive victories like the Battle of Gaugamela, his actions consistently involved personal exposure and aggressive maneuvering that a cautious commander would avoid.[3][5]

To a reasonable man, these decisions are indefensible. They violate expected value, risk management, and preservation instincts. Any MBA will suggest he was a mad man.

That is precisely the point.

Alexander was not optimizing within the system. He behaved as if the system did not apply to him. Maybe Olympias did convince him he was the son of Zeus. Was is true though? Does not mateter. Alexander behaved as if it were and in doing so, he bent reality around that assumption. He took a myth and enforced it, decision by decision, battle by battle, until the world had no choice but to reflect it back.

No one gave Alexander his divinity.

He took it.

### The war of the Chiefs.

What is a Ceo's duty? That is a pretty key question when talking about a start-up. To understand it, we need to talk first about what is a start-up and the role of the Ceo's biggest enemy: His CTO.

A start-up is like a bycicle but going down a Mount, the faster the bycicle go, its less likely you are going to fall. At the same time if you crush it will be "worse". The truth of the matter is, if you go slow you are garantee to fall, not even the expert cycler is able to handle a bike going slow. On the other end, going fast is scary and is also quite likely to crush. You will have a chance though. NEVER drive slowly your bike. It's garantee failure.

Usually, this "bike" its not driven by only one pilot. On the contrary, its by two: The archetipical CEO and its corresponding CTO. We will analyse this case first, as is the most common in the 21st century, although there are more options—see [Founders](#founders) *(planned)*.

So, how can a Bike on a hill be driven by two guys? Should they aim for the same like lovers that always agree? Or, on the contrary, are the enemys at war? 

In the methaphore, the CEO should be something like the strong muscle legs of our cyclist. He is ambicious and strong, so naturally he will pedal as fast as posible. 

The CTO, on the countrary, is the eyes of the cyclist and sees a lot of spike bushes, trees and even rocks on his way. So, naturally he will be scared of the way down the hill (as he should) and will yell everytime he stuff like "Be careful!", "Slow down!", "I cant evade that tree fast enough" and "If we sont slow down we will crush with that rock inevitably".

So, as you can see, the should fight a lot. They must. If the ambicious muscles cycle too fast the fear of the CTO will indeed come to life so fats he is unable to react and both die. There is a catch though, they may not know it (although they should) but they are not the only cyclist in that hill. There are many of them. And usually only the first to arrive at the fertile valley made of candy, sugar and flowers is the one that gets ALL the candy. Or, do you remember the second fully-working social network? Or the second search engine to not just filter by keywords? Me neither.

In my opinion, in the War of the chiefs the CEO MUST win. but only slightly. If you are a CTO, try to find a Bull as a CEO. He may be almost uncontrollable. But remember the almost part, because you are the only brakes in the car. On the contrary if your are searching for your CTO you must be able to win 80% of the times. But if he is unable to tame the rabious irrational dog you are once in a while, you are likely to bite bigger bones you can handle right now.

The perfect team works like a beautiful rally car on full speed to win the race. The CTO yells "hard left on 200m!" and the CEO, without releasing the accelerator in the slightlest, trust its chief and makes the full turn in the 200° turn like its life depends on it. And only if 100% necesary, the CTO should say the prohibed words "Brake! U turn to the right in 120!" And his CEO, with anger, should actually listen. [link to a race of rally, linked].

PD: Obviously, this two figures should be in the abstract, not in the concrete. If you are a solo founder, think of it as two forces inside you. The same for teams of three.You could even be the CTO and work at heart as the accelerar. Etc. 

## Chapter 2

### CEO double click. Agility?

So, as is clear in [Chapter 1](#chapter-1), a CEO should try to move fast. But, what does that even mean?

I could send an empty email in like 2 seconds. Is that good? is it bad? I dont know. Is not clear. First, we need to define a previous concept: Vision.

A good founder needs to try to get the team or institution somewhere. Anywhere. But never everywhere. Bad example: "we want to be the best e-commerce in the world with a uber-like app inside and a search engine with AI inside that..." yeah, sure honey. Better example: "We will create wheels for trucks with this material recently discovered. We will start with coast to coast large truck companies in USA as they really would benefit with our product".

To be fair, founders tends to be super ambicious, creative ans thrive in caotic industries. For that reason, sometimes their "Vision" has to be vague or abstract. Which is not a bad thing. The bad thing is that your milestones or objectives ar also vague, bad define or too abstract.

Once our good founder has a clear, not vague, milestone in some time frame. He needs to align the team towards that goal. aligning teams is actually a super hard thing to do. Because everyone has diferent opinions, distractions and subsets of information/beliefs. If you go fast, without aligment, its like a boat where everyone row randomly. What you want is something closer to a trirreme perfectly sinc at the same time, where you a nice compas and map, the team beliefs in you and you have a huge drum sounding "BUM, BUM, BUM" every 1,5 seconds and EVERYONE rows at the same time at the same direction.

What you want to maximize is: the speed of the rowers and the aligment between them. In a formula could be something close to Speed * Aligment. Its almost as vectors moving your boat. If every vector is pointing randomly you will barely move. On the same way, if every vector is align but they are very small you will go sloowly to your goal. In mathy way of puting it: you want to maximize the sum of the dot products of the vectors with respect to the direction your vision.

This new quantity, we will call it from now on: Agility. (If someone has a better name I am open to it. I am considering impact too or Impact on the goal)

### Your single most scarse resourse: You.

The single most valuable resource in every start-up is not its funding, dev team, marketing outbound or prestiege.

If a gene can give you only one wish and you choose to have all the funding in the world at your disposal absoluting nothing will move. Not until you can choose what to do with the funding. If you choose to have 100 expert engineers your problems will still be there (with extra costs actually). This proves a core idea, there is nothing more scarse in your project than your self, your attention, your speed to take decisions and execute them. The perfect wish would be to change today 24 hours for a 24000 hour day with full attention on the project. And I garantee you, all your problems would be solved.

### The problem of the dictator

Your company is like a Cerberus of problems. Every single time you solve a problem 3 new ones will arrive. If you improve your operations, now your mareting is the bottle neck, when that is solve you sales culture becomes trash, when you tackle that your product is the new issue, this process is infinite.

If you are a dictator, and everyone in the company needs your brain to take decisions you will never scale. never. not in a million years. The Dictatorship approach will never work because you can only solve 2 maybe 3 issueas a day. Not 10 nor 20. Bigger companies can produce 100 issueas an hour. Good luck with that.

### The solutions to agility: Army (better than dictatorship).

In my opinion one solution is raw hierarchy with full ownership (this is another important concept btw). An army has clearly defined levels of command, and everly level should interct mostly with their own level, the one bellow and the one on top.

The highest level takes all the Macro and strategic decisions. For example, if the general of the army wants to sail tomorrow, he wont ask the opinion of captain of the boat. In fact he wont interact with him at all. Not even to give him the order. He will just say to his subcommander "We will sail tomorrow" and expect perfect execution of that huge task. The subcommander will instantly delegate smaller tasks untill the captain of the boat delegates to the other sailers of hit ship to hoist the sail and so on. The information will flow back level by level until the general know we are good to sail. 

If for somereason a captain does not do his job. The general will instantly punish his subcommander, but not the captain. Because the ownership, aswell ad the information, always goes one level down.

They have to figure out that themseves.

Think of a pencil. Would you be able to create such product on your own?

o single person in the world knows how to make a pencil.

A pencil looks trivial: wood, graphite, a bit of rubber, some paint. But when you stop and trace its origins, it becomes astonishingly complex. The wood may come from trees cut down in one part of the world, using saws made of steel forged elsewhere. The graphite is mined, processed, and mixed with clay through techniques that require specialized knowledge. The rubber might originate from plantations in Southeast Asia. The metal band, the glue, the lacquer—all of it involves different materials, different processes, and different people spread across continents.

No one person oversees all of this. No central authority possesses all the knowledge required to coordinate every step. And yet, somehow, pencils are produced in massive quantities and sold for almost nothing.
